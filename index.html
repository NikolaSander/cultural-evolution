What Big Open Data Reveal About the Cultural Evolution

by Nikola Sander, Vienna Institute of Demography

A new study by Maximilian Schich and colleagues published in Science on August 1 traces the cultural evolution in Europe and North America over the last two centuries. The authors draw on three databases (Freebase and two datasets on artists) to map the locations of birth and death of more than 150,000 notable people.

I am intrigued by their findings and hopeful that our interactive circular visualisation of the Freebase dataset, which the authors kindly made available with their paper, gives a complementary view of the cultural evolution across space and time.

One Open Dataset - Two Visualisations

In their article titled "A network framework of cultural history", Maximilian Schich and his colleagues visualised the cultural evolution using network flow maps. I find the printed images of the large cultural network a little hard to read. The very large number of arcs between nodes makes it difficult to tease out the main corridors of migration from place of birth to place of death and how they changed over time.

The network visualisation works very well when using moving images. The excellent video embedded in a story by Alison Abbott on nature.com nicely visualises the large cultural network. In the video, the visual complexity is reduced by effective narration and a focus on one region at a time.

Kara Manke from npr.org introduced me to the work by Schich et al. Her story, for which she asked me for comments, was published in the 'Goast and Soda' blog. I thought that the circular migration plot we had developed for visualising our new migration estimates could provide some additional insight into the global patterns of cultural evolution.

Both visualisations have their advantages and disadvantages. Network flow maps give a clear sense of location and direction, and changes over time can be revealed effectively using animation. But it can be difficult to visualise complex and dynamic networks without overwhelming the map. Circular plots cannot provide the locational detail of an (animated) network map, but they effectively summarise all flows in the system and allow direct comparisons of the sizes of flows.

The evolution of culture from Europe (esp. Rome) to North America, and from the east to the west coast is shown very well in the map and in the plot. But in addition to this shift, the circular plot reveals surprisingly little migration from France during the French Revolution, and a stronger focus on the U.S. as a destination of notable people than, for example, the seminal works by Charles Joseph Minard on world migration in the 19th century suggest. A reflection of selectivity bias?
